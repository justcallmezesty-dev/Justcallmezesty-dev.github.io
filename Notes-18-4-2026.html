Got it. Throwing hyper-realistic Quixel Megascans and Kitbash3D models directly into a stylized, fast-paced environment is a recipe for visual noise. If we are completely ditching post-processing so you can mix styles seamlessly, and we definitely aren't hand-painting hundreds of assets, we need a technical, procedural pipeline.
As a programmer, you can tackle this by intercepting the art at two levels: processing the raw textures before they hit the material, and then using a robust UE5 Master Material to handle the shading and edges.
Here is your ultimate blueprint for a global, non-post-process painterly pipeline in Unreal Engine 5.
Step 1: The Compute Shader Texture Processor (The Megascans Fixer)
Megascans have too much micro-detail. To fix this without opening Substance Painter, we process the textures procedurally.
• Runtime Processing: You can write a compute shader that is called at the start of the game. This shader analyzes your Megascans or Kitbash albedo textures and averages out the colors within predefined "strokes".
• The Handoff: Once processed, the compute shader feeds this newly simplified, stylized texture directly to your fragment shader (your material).
• Why this rocks: This allows for the instant use of any high-fidelity asset pack with little to no artist input. It works brilliantly on uniquely unwrapped meshes.
Step 2: The Stylized Master Material (The Paint Behavior)
Now that your textures are simplified, you need a Master Material to make them behave like wet paint reacting to light.
• The Single "Brush" Texture: Create one utility texture packed with a blurred brush shape and grunge (Diffuse), a highly randomized normal map representing brush directions, and a detail mask.
• Cheap Depth: Run your base texture through a BumpOffset node using the brush height map. This gives your procedural brush strokes a thick, impasto depth.
• "Oily" Reflections: To make the paint feel alive, use the dot product of the light vector and reflection vector to lerp your specular colors. To prevent the reflections from perfectly matching the diffuse strokes, swizzle your texture coordinates (pivoting the normal map). As the player moves through the level, the colors will "dance," feeling liquid and oily.
Step 3: World-Space Edges & Shadows (Grounding the Assets)
Without a post-process outline filter, you need the material to handle occlusion and edge wear dynamically.
• SDF Occlusion: Since you're in UE5, tap into Lumen's Signed Distance Fields (SDF). You can use ApproxAO eroded by your brush texture's alpha channel. This gives you deep, painterly shadows in the crevices of your Kitbash buildings without baking ambient occlusion.
• Camera-Aligned Rim Erosion: You want the edges of your models to look "chipped" by brush strokes. Instead of standard screen-space projection (which breaks down and looks like a static overlay when the player pans the camera), use the Camera Vector's X and Y components. This creates a pseudo-view mapping that stays static when pivoting but reacts nicely when looking around.
• Fixing Edge Artifacts: Standard edge detection (like a cheap Fresnel) breaks horribly on flat surfaces. To fix this, bake the smooth normals of your meshes into their Vertex Colors before importing them. You can then write a small function in your shader using DDX and DDY to compute face curvature. This allows your shader to mask out flat faces and only apply the erosion to actual edges.
Step 4: Geometry-Based Outlines
If you still want a stylized outline without post-processing, use the inverted-hull method directly inside the asset setup.
• Instancing: Duplicate the mesh or use instances.
• Inflating: Use the smooth normals you baked into the Vertex Colors (from Step 3) to inflate the mesh outward flawlessly, even on sharp Kitbash geometry.
• Inverting: Use a two-sided masked material and utilize the TwoSidedSign flag to mask the front faces, effectively creating your outline. You can even add a random offset to the instances to make the outline feel like messy, overlapping brush strokes.

To implement Step 1—the Compute Shader Texture Processor—you are essentially building a custom utility that "pre-paints" your high-resolution textures (Megascans/Kitbash3D) the moment they are loaded or during a specialized bake step in the editor.
Because standard Megascans textures are too sharp and photorealistic, this shader smashes those fine details into clustered, painterly shapes before the material even renders them.
1. The Core Architecture
You need to create a "Texture Stylizer" system that consists of three parts:
• A Stroke Guide Map: A procedural texture (created in Substance Designer) that acts as a blueprint for the "brush strokes." It should contain randomized shapes and directional data.
• The HLSL Compute Shader (.usf): The logic that reads your Albedo, compares it to the Stroke Guide, and averages the colors.
• The Dispatcher: A small C++ class or a Blueprint script using the Render Dependency Graph (RDG) to run the shader and output a new texture.
2. The Compute Shader Logic (The "Stroke Averaging" Kernel)
The goal is to eliminate micro-detail while keeping the overall color and silhouette of the Megascans asset.
What you need to write:
Your shader should use a technique called K-Means Clustering or a Simplified Mean Shift.
• Input: The high-res Albedo and your "Stroke Guide" texture.
• Kernel Search: For every pixel, the shader looks at a neighborhood (e.g., 5x5 or 7x7 pixels).
• Stroke Weighting: Instead of a simple blur, it looks at the Stroke Guide. If a neighboring pixel falls within the same "shape" as the current pixel in the Guide Map, it gets a high weight.
• Averaging: It calculates the average color of all pixels in that neighborhood that share the same "stroke identity."
• Output: It writes this averaged, flat-colored "blob" to a UTextureRenderTarget2D.
3. Implementation in UE5
To make this work without a post-process filter, follow these technical steps:
A. The Shader File (.usf)
Create a file in your project’s Shaders directory. The kernel logic should look something like this:
• Use RWTexture2D for the output (the stylized texture).
• Use Texture2D for the input (the Megascans Albedo).
• Use a SamplerState to allow the shader to grab the "Stroke Guide."
B. The Shader Dispatcher
In Unreal, Compute Shaders don't just "run"; they must be dispatched to the GPU.
• Global Shader approach: Create a C++ class inheriting from FGlobalShader. This allows you to call your "Paint" function from anywhere in the engine.
• RDG (Render Dependency Graph): Use the RDG to manage the memory. This ensures that the texture processing happens at the right time in the frame (usually at the very beginning) so there’s no flickering.
C. The "Global" Automation Script
To avoid doing this manually for every rock and building:
• Create a Blueprint Utility or an Editor Utility Widget.
• Write a script that iterates through your "Selected Assets."
• For each asset, it grabs the Diffuse/Albedo texture.
• It creates a transient Render Target of the same resolution.
• It dispatches the Compute Shader to "Paint" the Albedo into the Render Target.
• It then creates a "Stylized Material Instance" that uses this new Render Target instead of the original texture.
4. Handling the "No Post-Process" Constraint
Since you aren't using a post-process filter, your Master Material needs to be ready to receive these textures.
• Input Switch: Build a "Stylized" boolean into your Master Material. When true, it switches the Texture Sample from the standard Megascans file to the processed "Painterly" Render Target.
• Normal Map Simplification: Do not forget to run your Normal Maps through the same Compute Shader. If you have "painterly" colors but "photorealistic" bumpy normals, the effect will break. Averaging the normals creates the look of thick, flat paint strokes that catch the light in chunks.
Why this works for Kitbash3D and Megascans:
Kitbash3D models often have very clean, "CG" looking geometry. By processing the textures this way, you introduce "accidental" color variety and stroke-like edges that break up the perfect 3D lines. Because this happens at the texture level, it remains fully compatible with Lumen, shadows, and reflections without the "jitter" often associated with screen-space post-process filters.





To ground high-fidelity assets like Megascans and Kitbash3D in a painterly world, you have to break the "perfect" CG shadows and edges. Since we aren't using a post-process filter, we must build this logic into the Master Material so every object handles its own occlusion and edge-wear procedurally.
Here is the technical breakdown of how to build the grounding systems for Step 3.
1. Clumpy SDF Ambient Occlusion
Standard Ambient Occlusion (AO) is too smooth and realistic. Painterly shadows should look like a dark wash of paint that has "pooled" in the crevices.
• The Data Source: Use UE5’s Global Distance Fields. In the material, use the DistanceToNearestSurface node.
• The Logic: 1. Normalize the distance field data to create a mask where the crevices are white and the open surfaces are black.
2. The Painterly Twist: Do not use this mask directly. Multiply it by the Alpha Channel of your Packed Stroke Texture (from Step 2).
3. This "erodes" the shadow. Instead of a smooth gradient in a corner, the shadow will follow the shape of your brush strokes.
• Implementation: Use this clumpy mask to lerp the Base Color toward a deeper, more saturated version of itself (mimicking a "pigment pool") rather than just multiplying by black.
2. The "Smooth-Normal" Edge Mask
To add edge highlights or "chipped paint" effects, you need to tell the shader exactly where the sharp edges of a Kitbash building are. Standard Fresnel is view-dependent and "glowy"—we want something static and structural.
• The Preparation: In Blender (or via a script), you must bake Smooth Normals into the Vertex Colors (typically the RGB channels). This creates a "fake" version of the mesh where every edge is perfectly rounded.
• The Logic: In your UE5 material, compare the Pixel Normal (the actual geometry) with the Vertex Color (your baked smooth normals) using a Dot Product:
$$\text{EdgeMask} = 1.0 - \text{saturate}(\vec{N}_{pixel} \cdot \vec{V}_{color})$$
• The Result: This math yields a value of 0 on flat faces (where the normals match) and a value approaching 1 at sharp edges (where they differ).
• Refinement: Run this mask through a Power node to sharpen it, then use it as a lerp factor for edge highlights.
3. Camera-Aligned Rim Erosion
You want the silhouettes of your Megascans rocks to look like they were "cut" with a palette knife. Using standard UVs for this looks "tiled," and using screen-space textures causes "swimming" (the texture moves when the camera pans).
• The Logic: Project your brush texture onto the object using the Camera Vector.
• The Execution: 1. Take the Camera Vector node and use a Component Mask to grab only the R and G (X and Y).
2. Multiply this by a "Scale" parameter to control stroke size.
3. Use this as the UV input for your stroke texture.
• The Result: Because it is based on the camera’s orientation, the brush strokes stay "flat" relative to your screen, but they are pinned to the 3D surface. When you pan the camera, the strokes feel like they are part of the object’s paint layer, but they maintain a consistent "hand-drawn" scale.
4. Face Curvature via DDX/DDY
For Kitbash3D models, which often have massive flat surfaces, you can add "accidental" color variety (color jitter) to stop them from looking like grey slabs.
• The Logic: Use the HLSL functions ddx and ddy to calculate the change in surface normal across pixels.
• The Math: By taking the cross product of the partial derivatives of the world position, you can find the "flatness" or "curvature" of a surface at the pixel level.
• The Implementation: 1. Use this curvature value to drive a small Hue Shift.
2. Even a $2\% \text{ to } 5\%$ shift in hue across a large flat wall—masked by a noise texture—mimics the way a painter mixes slightly different blues and greys into a single stroke to keep the "canvas" interesting.
Summary of what you need to make:
• A "Smooth-Normal" script: For Blender or UE5 to store averaged normals in Vertex Color.
• The Edge-Shadow Function: A Material Function that combines the SDF mask and the Smooth-Normal Dot Product.
• The Projection Node: A specific section of your Master Material that uses the Camera Vector to drive the stroke mask.
By grounding the assets this way, the Kitbash3D buildings won't look like floating 3D models; they will appear to have been "built" with thick, physical paint that responds to the environment's light and depth.

Since you aren't using a post-process filter, Geometry-Based Outlines (also known as the "Inverted Hull" or "Shell" method) are the most effective way to get clean, stylized lines on your Kitbash3D and Megascans assets.
The challenge with standard assets is that they have sharp, high-poly edges. If you just "duplicate and flip" the mesh, the outline will look like a thin, clinical CG line. To make it look painterly, we need to use Vertex Color expansion and Procedural Alpha Masking.
1. The Core Concept: The Inverted Hull
The goal is to create a second "shell" of geometry around your model that is slightly larger than the original, colored black, and rendered inside-out.
• The Mesh: You don't need to manually duplicate the mesh in your 3D software. You can use Geometry Scripting in UE5 or simply add a second Material Slot to your assets.
• The Material Logic: The shader will push the vertices of this second slot outward along their normals and only render the "back" of the faces.
2. The "Smooth Normal" Secret (Critical for Kitbash3D)
If you expand a sharp building corner using standard normals, the outline will "break" at the seams. To fix this, you must use the Smooth Normals we discussed in Step 3.
• Preparation: Ensure your mesh has Averaged/Smooth Normals baked into its Vertex Colors (RGB).
• In UE5: In your Outline Material, instead of using the Vertex Normal WS node, you will pull the Vertex Color node.
• Expansion: Multiply the Vertex Color by an "Outline Width" parameter and plug it into the World Position Offset (WPO) pin.
• Why this works: Using smoothed normals ensures the "outline shell" expands like a perfectly inflated balloon, keeping the lines continuous even on the sharpest 90-degree corners of a Kitbash building.
3. Creating the "Painterly" Stroke Breakup
A solid black line looks like a cartoon; we want it to look like a messy brush stroke.
• The Mask: Use the Alpha Channel of your Packed Stroke Texture (from Step 2).
• The Logic: 1. Project the Stroke Texture onto the outline shell using World-Aligned Textures (so the stroke size stays consistent regardless of the building's scale).
2. Subtract this mask from the Opacity of your outline.
3. The Result: This "erodes" the outline. Instead of a solid line, it will look like a charcoal sketch or a dry brush stroke that fades in and out along the edges of the model.
4. The Two-Sided Rendering Setup
To make the outline work correctly without a post-process pass, your material needs specific settings:
• Material Domain: Surface.
• Blend Mode: Masked (or Translucent if you want soft brush edges).
• Shading Model: Unlit (Outlines shouldn't receive 3D lighting; they should be a flat color).
• Two-Sided: Checked. * The "Inside-Out" Trick: In the material graph, use the TwoSidedSign node. Multiply your opacity by this node. This ensures the shader masks out the "front" faces of the shell and only renders the "back" faces—effectively showing you the "inside" of the larger shell behind your model.
5. Implementation Steps: What to build
• The Outline Master Material: Create a material with the "Two-Sided Unlit" settings described above. Add a Scalar Parameter for "Outline Thickness" and "Stroke Intensity."
• The Asset Setup:
• Open your Megascans or Kitbash mesh.
• Add a new Material Slot.
• Apply your Outline Master Material to this second slot.
• Note: If the asset is a "Nanite" mesh, ensure "Programmable Rasterizer" is enabled in the material, or WPO might not behave as expected.
• Global Control: Create a Material Parameter Collection (MPC). Link the "Outline Thickness" in your Master Material to a global variable in the MPC.
• The Perk: You can now change the "sketchiness" of the entire game world—making the outlines thicker or thinner—instantly via Blueprint or Sequencer.
By using this geometry-based approach, you get outlines that are "pinned" to the objects. They won't jitter like post-process lines, and they will accurately follow the complex geometry of your Kitbash sets while maintaining the "hand-painted" texture of your brush strokes
